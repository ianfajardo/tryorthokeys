## What Is a Staggered Keyboard?

A **staggered keyboard** is the layout almost everyone learned to type on. The keys are offset row-by-row in a specific pattern — the second row is shifted slightly right of the top, the third row shifted further still. This is sometimes called "row-staggered" to distinguish it from column-staggered alternatives.

The reason for the offset isn't ergonomic. It's mechanical. Original typewriters had levers underneath each key, and the levers needed space to swing without colliding. Staggering the rows gave each lever its own column to move through. The layout stuck through the transition to electric typewriters, then keyboards, then modern computers — long after the mechanical reason for it disappeared.

That history is the whole point: **staggered keyboards are staggered because of 19th-century mechanical constraints, not because the layout is ergonomically optimal.**

## What Is an Ortholinear Keyboard?

An **ortholinear keyboard** uses a perfect grid layout. Every key is the same size (usually 1u, or 1 unit wide), and every column is a straight vertical line. No row-to-row offset, no staggered shifts — just a clean grid.

The "ortho" part of the name means "perpendicular" or "at right angles." Each column meets each row at exactly 90 degrees, which is also how the bones in your fingers actually move — straight up and down, not at the diagonal angle a staggered keyboard forces them into.

Most ortholinear keyboards are also small. Common sizes are **40%** (around 47 keys, like the [OLKB Planck](/olkb) or [Chosfox Geonix Rev.2](/keyboards#chosfox-geonix-rev2)) and **50%** (around 59 keys, like the [Preonic](/olkb) or [MechDIY 59](/mechdiy-59)). The compact size pairs naturally with the grid — fewer keys means tighter columns means a more focused typing experience.

> **Quick note on terminology:** "Ortholinear" and "ortho" are used interchangeably. "Ortholinear keyboard" and "ortho keyboard" mean the same thing.

## The Real Trade-Offs

Comparison tables look clean, but real-world decisions hit messier territory. Here's what actually matters when you're choosing between the two.

### 1. The Learning Curve Is Real (But Short)

If you're a competent touch typist on a staggered board, your first day on an ortho keyboard will feel like learning to type all over again. Your fingers will hit the wrong keys constantly because they're trained to reach diagonally. Expect a 30–50% drop in typing speed for the first 2–3 days.

By day 7, most typists are back to about 80% of their normal speed. By week 2–3, most are at parity or slightly faster. The retraining is real but short.

If you can't afford to be slow at work for a week, this is a meaningful constraint. If you can absorb it, the long-term gain is worth the short-term pain.

### 2. Ortho Is More Comfortable, But Only If You Type a Lot

Ortholinear's ergonomic advantage is most visible during long typing sessions — writing code, drafting documents, anything where you type for hours at a stretch. The straight vertical columns mean your fingers move up and down rather than at an angle, which over time reduces strain on the wrist tendons.

If you're mostly using your computer to browse, watch videos, and occasionally type a message, you'll feel the difference much less. The ergonomic case for ortho is strongest for programmers, writers, and anyone with existing wrist or RSI concerns. For casual users, the upside is smaller.

### 3. Compatibility Friction Is Almost Always Underestimated

Switching to ortho means you'll occasionally type on someone else's staggered keyboard — at a colleague's desk, on a laptop at an airport, on a friend's setup. The retraining works both ways, and your fingers will get confused for a few minutes each time. Most people adjust quickly, but it's a real cost.

The same applies if you frequently switch between an ortho board and a laptop's built-in (staggered) keyboard. For laptop-heavy users, this is the single biggest argument against full ortho commitment.

### 4. Keycap Compatibility Is a Hidden Tax

Most off-the-shelf keycap sets are designed for staggered layouts and ship with 1.25u, 1.5u, 2.25u, and other non-1u modifiers. On an ortho board where every key is 1u, those modifiers don't fit. You either need:

- A keycap set that explicitly includes 1u modifier kits (Drop MT3 sets used to, sets like [Pimp My Keyboard's DSA sets](/keycaps#pimpmykeyboard) still do), or
- A dedicated ortho-friendly profile like DSA where every key is the same shape

Our [keycaps page](/keycaps) lists current ortho-compatible sets. The short version: budget about 20–30% more for a good keycap set on an ortho board than on a staggered one, just because the inventory is smaller.

## Who Should Choose Ortholinear?

You'll get the most out of an ortholinear keyboard if you:

- **Type for hours daily** — programmers, writers, transcriptionists, customer support
- **Have wrist or RSI concerns** — the straight columns reduce ulnar deviation
- **Want a small, portable keyboard** — 40% and 50% ortho boards are extremely compact
- **Enjoy tinkering** — most ortho boards run QMK or VIA and reward customization
- **Don't switch between many keyboards** — full commitment beats half-commitment

## Who Should Stick With Staggered?

Staggered is the right call if you:

- **Switch between laptops and external keyboards constantly** — the muscle-memory conflict isn't worth it
- **Game competitively** — most game UIs and tutorials assume staggered layouts
- **Type infrequently** — the ergonomic gain doesn't apply if you're not at the keyboard for hours
- **Need full keyboards (full-size, TKL) for work** — ortho is dominated by 40% and 50% sizes
- **Share your computer with others** — non-ortho users will struggle on an ortho board

## FAQ: Ortholinear vs Staggered

### Is an ortholinear keyboard faster than a staggered keyboard?

After the learning curve, most typists land at about the same speed or slightly faster on an ortholinear keyboard. The gain isn't huge — typically 5–10% — but it's measurable. The bigger long-term benefit is reduced finger and wrist strain, not raw words-per-minute.

### How long does it take to learn an ortholinear keyboard?

Most touch typists hit 80% of their normal speed within a week, and full parity within 2–3 weeks. Expect significant errors for the first 2–3 days as muscle memory rebuilds.

### Can I use any keycap set on an ortholinear keyboard?

Most MX-compatible keycap sets will fit the switches on an ortho board, but the included key sizes are usually designed for staggered layouts. For a 40% or 50% ortho board you specifically want a set that includes 1u modifiers (or a uniform-profile set like DSA where every key is the same shape). See our [ortholinear keycaps page](/keycaps).

### Are ortholinear keyboards good for gaming?

For most non-competitive gaming, yes. Movement keys (WASD or layered alternatives) work just as well on a grid layout. For competitive FPS players, the learning curve and the lack of a number row on 40% boards is a real adjustment — most pros stay on staggered.

### Are split keyboards always ortholinear?

No — split keyboards split a keyboard into two halves, but each half can be either staggered or ortho. Examples of staggered splits include the Keychron Q11 and the Kinesis Advantage 2. Examples of ortho splits include the ZSA Voyager, Dygma Defy, and DIY boards like the Corne and Sofle. See our [split keyboards page](/split) for the full comparison.

### What is column-staggered vs row-staggered?

Row-staggered is the traditional layout where each row shifts horizontally relative to the row above it — this is what most retail keyboards use. Column-staggered (also called "true ergonomic") is a hybrid where columns are shifted vertically to match different finger lengths. Most modern ergo splits like the Glove80 and Dygma Defy use column-staggered layouts. Pure ortholinear (Planck, Geonix) uses neither — every column is perfectly aligned.
